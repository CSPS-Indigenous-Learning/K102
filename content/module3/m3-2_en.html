Red River Resistance

The Red River Resistance of 1869–1870 was sparked by the transfer of Rupert's Land to Canada.

A colony of farmers and hunters, many of them Métis, occupied a corner of Rupert's Land and feared for their culture and land rights under the control of the Government of Canada. Métis people resisted and declared a provisional government to negotiate. This resulted in the passing of the Manitoba Act, 1870 and the creation of the province of Manitoba.

Section 31 of the Manitoba Act, 1870 included a provision for Métis grants. This provision led Prime Minister Sir John A. Macdonald to articulate a view that would begin the process of denying Métis identity for the next century:

“If they are Indians, they go with the tribe; if they are half-breeds they are whites.”
