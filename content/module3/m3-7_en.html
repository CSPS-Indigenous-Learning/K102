Constitution Act, 1982

Over the years, when the Government of Canada developed policies and programs for First Nations and Inuit, it did not take Métis people into consideration.

Only a few government policies focused specifically on the Métis.

Métis people often found themselves in a position where they were forced to:

  • deny their identity and pretend to be European settlers OR
  • stay true to their identity and face hostility and discrimination from society

In 1982, the Government of Canada patriated its Constitution from Britain and brought it to Canada. This changed the course of history for the Métis. The Constitution Act, 1982 included a number of amendments to the Constitution of Canada, notably the addition of section 35. Section 35 recognizes and affirms existing Aboriginal rights, such as treaty rights, for First Nations, Métis and Inuit.

“The greatest challenge the Métis face is that there is no legislation regarding the Métis, nor is there a consistent, system-wide government policy on how to work with the Métis.”

Manitoba Métis Federation

In 1981, the Manitoba Métis Federation wanted to prove that by implementing the Manitoba Act, 1870, the Government of Canada breached fiduciary obligations owed to Métis and that certain legislation passed by the province of Manitoba affected the implementation of the Manitoba Act.

The Supreme Court of Canada held that the Government of Canada failed to implement the land grant provisions set out in section 31 of the Manitoba Act, 1870, in a manner consistent with the honour of the Crown. 

This decision permitted the Manitoba Métis Federation to bring forth the claim on behalf of the descendants of Métis that were affected by the failure of the Crown as “the body representing the collective Métis interest.” It created a precedent for the Manitoba Métis Federation and other Métis National Council Affiliates as rights-bearing Métis collectives.

The case was initially launched in 1981. A final decision was rendered in 2013—a total of 32 years in court!

Daniels, Supreme Court of Canada, 2016

In Daniels v Canada, the Supreme Court of Canada ruled that Métis and Non-Status Indians are “Indians” for purposes of the Constitution.

This determined that, under section 91(24) of the Constitution Act, 1867, the Government of Canada had legislative authority with respect to Métis people.

While the 2016 decision has not yet been fully implemented, its immediate impact is that Métis are now officially recognized as Indians. This does not mean that Métis fall under the Indian Act or other programs that are specific to First Nations or Inuit.

The situation of Métis under the Canadian Constitution has changed many times since Canada came into being. The Métis are currently working hard with the Government of Canada to find their appropriate position in the legislation.
