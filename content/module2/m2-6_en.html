Getting to know a little about Métis culture

Generally speaking, Métis are community-centred people with a rich and varied culture and many traditions. Historically and today, many Métis people celebrate individual and communal achievements during the “rendezvous”. The rendezvous is a celebratory event that includes community suppers, dances such as the Red River Jig and distinctive Métis fiddling.

Here are a few iconic Métis cultural symbols — though not all Métis will identify with them. There are many more to discover!

Métis flag

The Métis flag is one of the oldest patriotic flags in Canada.

It was first used in 1816 by Métis resistance fighters before the Battle of Seven Oaks.

The Métis Nation has had a number of flags over the years, but the blue background with the white infinity symbol is its most recognized emblem of nationhood.

The white symbol has two meanings for the Métis: the joining of two cultures and the existence of a people forever.

This blue and white flag is still flown today by the Métis Nation.

Métis anthem

Métis have a unique anthem representing their culture and history.

Among Indigenous peoples in Canada, they are the only group with an anthem.

We invite you to visit the Gabriel Dumont Institute website and listen to this beautiful anthem.

Beadwork

Métis people are famous for their floral beadwork.

It is a unique art form easily identified by intricately beaded floral patterns on a dark background.

Métis were often called “the flower beadwork people.”

Métis sash

The Métis sash is another symbol of Métis culture and history.

Historically, the Métis sash served as a temporary tumpline (strap), first-aid kit, washcloth, towel, sewing kit and emergency bridle or saddle blanket.

Today, the sash is worn by members of the Métis Nation as a symbol of nationhood and pride. Each colour in the sash has a specific meaning.
