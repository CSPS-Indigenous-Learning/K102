Who are Métis under the Canadian Constitution?

Since the Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples (RCAP) released its report in 1996, there have been developments in legally defining Métis under section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982.

R v Powley

From a political perspective, the Supreme Court of Canada’s decision in R v Powley is an important starting point to understand who Métis are. Although the case was specifically about hunting rights, many people recognize the Powley decision as a way to determine other rights and protections for Métis under the Canadian Constitution.

The 2003 decision provided a three-part test to identify Métis rights holders:

  • self-identification as Métis
  • ancestral connection to an historic Métis community
  • community acceptance

While this may provide a way of determining what rights and protections may be found under the Canadian Constitution, it does not provide a definition of who else may consider themselves Métis.

Métis Nation

The largest acknowledged Métis group under section 35(2) of the Constitution Act, 1982 is the Métis Nation, which dates back to the time of the fur trade. These Métis people generally reside in parts of Ontario and westward and share a common heritage, history, culture, language and political identity.

However, Métis communities exist elsewhere in Ontario and eastward. Many of them have made claims to the Government of Canada to also be recognized as official Métis groups in Canada.
